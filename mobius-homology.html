Simplicial Homology
& The Möbius Strip

How non-orientability, invisible to local smooth geometry, is encoded in the torsion of relative homology groups and the long exact sequence of a pair.

Topics: simplicial homology · boundary operators · long exact sequences · orientation sheaf · local vs. global topology
§1 Simplicial Complexes ▾
Definition — $n$-simplex

The standard $n$-simplex $\Delta^n$ is the convex hull of $n+1$ affinely independent points $v_0,\ldots,v_n$ in some $\mathbb{R}^N$. Concretely:

$$\Delta^n = \left\{\,\sum_{i=0}^n t_i v_i \;\middle|\; t_i \geq 0,\; \sum t_i = 1\,\right\}.$$

A face of $[v_0,\ldots,v_n]$ is any sub-simplex $[v_{i_0},\ldots,v_{i_k}]$ with $i_0 < \cdots < i_k$.

Definition — Simplicial Complex

A simplicial complex $K$ is a finite collection of simplices such that: (i) every face of a simplex in $K$ is in $K$, and (ii) the intersection of any two simplices is a face of each. The $n$-skeleton $K^{(n)}$ is the sub-complex of simplices of dimension $\leq n$.

An oriented $n$-simplex $[v_0,\ldots,v_n]$ is an $n$-simplex together with an equivalence class of orderings of its vertices under even permutations. Two orderings are equivalent if they differ by an even permutation; negating an oriented simplex corresponds to swapping two vertices. Thus $[v_0,v_1,v_2] = [v_1,v_2,v_0] = [v_2,v_0,v_1]$ but $[v_0,v_1,v_2] = -[v_1,v_0,v_2]$.

For 1-simplices (edges), an orientation is simply a direction: $[v_0,v_1]$ means the edge directed from $v_0$ to $v_1$, and $[v_1,v_0] = -[v_0,v_1]$. For 2-simplices (triangles), an orientation selects a rotational sense: counterclockwise or clockwise.

§2 Chain Complexes & Homology ▾
Definition — Chain Groups

The $n$-th chain group $C_n(K;\mathbb{Z})$ is the free abelian group on the set of oriented $n$-simplices of $K$ (with the relation that reversing orientation negates the generator). Elements are formal integer linear combinations $\sum_i a_i \sigma_i$ called $n$-chains.

Definition — Boundary Operator

The boundary map $\partial_n : C_n \to C_{n-1}$ is defined on a generator by

$$\partial_n [v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_n] = \sum_{i=0}^{n} (-1)^i\, [v_0, \ldots, \hat{v}_i, \ldots, v_n],$$

where $\hat{v}_i$ denotes omission of the $i$-th vertex. Extended linearly to all chains.

The fundamental property is $\partial_{n-1} \circ \partial_n = 0$ for all $n$ — the boundary of a boundary is zero. This is verified by a sign-cancellation argument: each codimension-2 face appears twice with opposite signs. The chain groups and boundary maps form a chain complex:

$$\cdots \xrightarrow{\partial_{n+1}} C_n \xrightarrow{\partial_n} C_{n-1} \xrightarrow{\partial_{n-1}} \cdots \xrightarrow{\partial_1} C_0 \xrightarrow{\partial_0 = 0} 0.$$
Definition — Homology Groups

The $n$-th homology group is the quotient

$$H_n(K;\mathbb{Z}) = \ker \partial_n \,/\, \operatorname{im} \partial_{n+1}.$$

Elements of $\ker \partial_n$ are called $n$-cycles; elements of $\operatorname{im} \partial_{n+1}$ are $n$-boundaries. Homology measures the cycles that are not boundaries — the "holes" in dimension $n$.

Intuition

$H_0$ counts path components. $H_1$ counts independent "loops that cannot be filled in." $H_2$ counts enclosed "voids." For surfaces with boundary, $H_2 = 0$ unless the surface encloses a 3-dimensional void (which requires it to be closed and orientable). Non-orientability will prevent the formation of an $H_2$ class, but this fact is not immediately obvious — it requires the long exact sequence to see cleanly.

Definition — Exact Sequence

A sequence of abelian groups and homomorphisms $\cdots \to A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C \to \cdots$ is exact at $B$ if $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. It is exact if exact at every term. Short exact sequences $0 \to A \to B \to C \to 0$ encode extensions: $C \cong B/A$.

§3 The Cylinder — A Reference Case ▾

The cylinder $C = S^1 \times [0,1]$ is our orientable reference. We triangulate it as a band of 6 triangles over 6 vertices. Label the bottom boundary circle $\{p, q, r\}$ and the top $\{a, b, c\}$, with the 6 triangles:

$$\tau_1=[p,q,b],\; \tau_2=[p,b,a],\; \tau_3=[q,r,c],\; \tau_4=[q,c,b],\; \tau_5=[r,p,a],\; \tau_6=[r,a,c].$$

The identifications for the cylinder close the band into an annulus: vertex $p$ connects back on both boundary circles. The chain data: $V=6$, $E=9$, $F=6$, giving $\chi = 6-9+6 = 3$...

Rather than compute this in full, the key result is:

Homology of the Cylinder
$$H_0(C) \cong \mathbb{Z}, \quad H_1(C) \cong \mathbb{Z}, \quad H_n(C) = 0 \text{ for } n \geq 2.$$

The cylinder deformation retracts to $S^1$ (collapse the $[0,1]$ factor), which has $H_0 = H_1 = \mathbb{Z}$ and higher homology zero. The boundary $\partial C = S^1 \sqcup S^1$ consists of two circles.

The generator of $H_1(C)$ is the class $[\alpha]$ of any horizontal circle. Each boundary component maps into $H_1(C)$ with degree $\pm 1$ — the two boundary circles are homotopic (parallel) circles on the cylinder. This will contrast sharply with the Möbius strip.

§4 Triangulating the Möbius Strip ▾

The Möbius strip is constructed from a rectangle by identifying opposite edges with a half-twist. Take $[0,3] \times [0,1]$ and impose the identification $(0, s) \sim (3, 1-s)$. This gives a compact surface with boundary.

Vertex identification

Place vertices on the rectangle:

  • Bottom row: $u_0=(0,0),\; u_1=(1,0),\; u_2=(2,0),\; u_3=(3,0)$
  • Top row: $v_0=(0,1),\; v_1=(1,1),\; v_2=(2,1),\; v_3=(3,1)$

The identification $(0,s)\sim(3,1-s)$ forces: $u_0 = v_3$ (set $s=0$) and $v_0 = u_3$ (set $s=1$).

After identification, the six distinct vertices are:

$$p := u_0 = v_3,\quad q := u_1,\quad r := u_2,\quad s := u_3 = v_0,\quad t := v_1,\quad u := v_2.$$

The six triangles

Subdivide each of the three $1\times 1$ columns of the rectangle into two triangles, oriented counterclockwise:

The identification at column 3 is where the twist enters: the right edge of the rectangle re-enters with vertices reversed ($u_3 = v_0 = s$ and $v_3 = u_0 = p$), so the triangle $[r, s, p]$ has $s$ at the top-left position and $p$ at the top-right — the reversal of what you'd expect for a cylinder.

$$\begin{align} \partial\sigma_1 &= \partial[p,q,t] = e_1 - e_4 + e_8 \\ \partial\sigma_2 &= \partial[p,t,s] = e_4 - e_3 - e_{11} \\ \partial\sigma_3 &= \partial[q,r,u] = e_6 - e_7 + e_{10} \\ \partial\sigma_4 &= \partial[q,u,t] = e_7 - e_8 - e_{12} \\ \partial\sigma_5 &= \partial[r,s,p] = e_9 + e_2 - e_3 \\ \partial\sigma_6 &= \partial[r,p,u] = {-e_2} - e_{10} + e_5 \end{align}$$

Note the sign in $\partial\sigma_5$: $[s,p] = -[p,s] = -e_3$ and $[r,p] = -[p,r] = -e_2$, so $\partial[r,s,p] = [r,s] - [r,p] + [s,p] = e_9 -(-e_2) + (-e_3) = e_9 + e_2 - e_3$. This encodes the twist.

§5 Computing Homology of the Möbius Strip ▾

$H_0$: path components

Since the Möbius strip is connected, $H_0(M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, generated by the class of any vertex.

$H_2$: cycles among the 2-chains

We ask: does $\ker \partial_2$ contain any non-zero element? Suppose $\sum a_i \sigma_i$ is a 2-cycle. Each edge that belongs to only one triangle must contribute a boundary term that cannot cancel; but every boundary edge of the strip (e.g. $e_1 = pq$, appearing only in $\sigma_1$) forces $a_1 = 0$, and by propagation all $a_i = 0$. Therefore:

$$H_2(M;\mathbb{Z}) = 0.$$

This has a clean conceptual explanation: any closed 2-chain on a non-orientable surface must vanish, because you cannot consistently orient all triangles so that internal edges cancel and boundary edges also cancel.

$H_1$: loops modulo boundaries

The Möbius strip deformation retracts onto its core circle — the circle running lengthwise along the center of the strip. (Define $r: M \to S^1$ by collapsing the width; this is a homotopy equivalence.) Therefore:

$$H_1(M;\mathbb{Z}) \cong H_1(S^1;\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z},$$

generated by the class $[\alpha]$ of the core circle. So the absolute homology of $M$ is indistinguishable from that of the cylinder — both look like $S^1$ algebraically.

Punchline

The Möbius strip and the open cylinder have identical homology groups: $(H_0, H_1, H_2) = (\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}, 0)$. To algebraically distinguish them, we must look at the relative homology and the long exact sequence of the pair $(X, \partial X)$.

The boundary circle

The boundary $\partial M$ is a single circle. Tracing through the triangulation: start at $p$, traverse bottom edges $p \to q \to r \to s$, then top edges $s \to t \to u \to p$ (the top runs in the same direction as the bottom because of the identification). The boundary 1-cycle is:

$$\gamma_{\partial} = e_1 + e_6 + e_9 + e_{11} + e_{12} - e_5.$$

One can verify $\partial_1 \gamma_\partial = 0$: the sum telescopes to zero. Now, crucially, what is the class $[\gamma_\partial]$ in $H_1(M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$?

Under the retraction $r: M \to S^1$, the boundary circle maps to the core circle with degree 2. This is the key topological fact: as you walk once around the boundary of the Möbius strip, you traverse the core twice — once on each "side" of the band (the two sides are, of course, connected). Therefore:

$$i_*([\gamma_\partial]) = 2[\alpha] \in H_1(M) \cong \mathbb{Z},$$

where $i: \partial M \hookrightarrow M$ is the inclusion. This factor of 2 is the algebraic footprint of the half-twist.

§6 Long Exact Sequence of the Pair $(X,\partial X)$ ▾

For any pair $(X, A)$ with $A \subseteq X$, the relative chain groups $C_n(X,A) = C_n(X)/C_n(A)$ fit into a long exact sequence:

$$\cdots \to H_n(A) \xrightarrow{i_*} H_n(X) \xrightarrow{j_*} H_n(X,A) \xrightarrow{\partial_*} H_{n-1}(A) \to \cdots$$

where $\partial_*$ is the connecting homomorphism. We apply this to the pair $(X, \partial X)$ for both our surfaces.

The Cylinder $(C, \partial C)$

Data: $\partial C = S^1 \sqcup S^1$, so $H_1(\partial C) = \mathbb{Z}^2$ and $H_0(\partial C) = \mathbb{Z}^2$. Also $H_k(C) = H_k(S^1)$ and $H_k(\partial C) = H_k(S^1) \oplus H_k(S^1)$. The inclusion $i_*: H_1(\partial C) = \mathbb{Z}^2 \to H_1(C) = \mathbb{Z}$ sends each boundary circle to the core circle: $i_*(a,b) = a+b$ (or $a-b$ depending on orientation choice — let's say the two boundary circles map with degrees $+1$ and $+1$, so $i_*(a,b) = a + b$... actually for a consistently oriented cylinder, one boundary maps with degree $+1$ and the other with degree $-1$, giving $i_*(a,b) = a - b$. The kernel of $a-b$ is $\{(a,a)\} \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

Writing out the relevant portion of the long exact sequence:

$$0 \to H_2(C, \partial C) \xrightarrow{\partial_*} \underbrace{H_1(\partial C)}_{\mathbb{Z}^2} \xrightarrow{i_* = (1,-1)} \underbrace{H_1(C)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \to H_1(C,\partial C) \to \underbrace{H_0(\partial C)}_{\mathbb{Z}^2} \xrightarrow{\cong} \underbrace{H_0(C)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \to 0$$

Reading off the groups:

  • From $0 \to H_2(C,\partial C) \to \mathbb{Z}^2$: the image of $H_2(C,\partial C)$ lands in $\ker(i_*) = \ker(a-b) = \{(a,a)\} \cong \mathbb{Z}$. So $H_2(C,\partial C) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
  • The map $\mathbb{Z}^2 \xrightarrow{a-b} \mathbb{Z}$ is surjective, so $\ker(H_1(C) \to H_1(C,\partial C)) = \mathbb{Z}$, meaning $H_1(C)$ maps to zero in $H_1(C,\partial C)$.
  • The image of $H_1(C,\partial C) \to H_0(\partial C)$ equals $\ker(H_0(\partial C) \to H_0(C)) = \ker((1,1)) = \{(a,-a)\} \cong \mathbb{Z}$. So $H_1(C,\partial C) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
Relative Homology — Cylinder
$$H_n(C, \partial C) \cong \begin{cases} 0 & n=0 \\ \mathbb{Z} & n=1 \\ \mathbb{Z} & n=2 \end{cases}$$

The class generating $H_2(C,\partial C) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ is the fundamental class $[C, \partial C]$, a manifestation of the cylinder's orientability. It is the algebraic version of "you can consistently orient the whole surface."

The Möbius Strip $(M, \partial M)$

Data: $\partial M = S^1$ (a single circle), so $H_1(\partial M) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $H_0(\partial M) = \mathbb{Z}$. The inclusion map $i_*: H_1(\partial M) \to H_1(M)$ sends the generator $[\gamma_\partial]$ to $2[\alpha]$, as we established. So $i_*$ is multiplication by 2:

$$i_*: \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \mathbb{Z}.$$

The relevant portion of the long exact sequence:

$$0 \to \underbrace{H_2(M,\partial M)}_{?} \xrightarrow{\partial_*} \underbrace{H_1(\partial M)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \underbrace{H_1(M)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \to \underbrace{H_1(M,\partial M)}_{?} \to \underbrace{H_0(\partial M)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\cong} \underbrace{H_0(M)}_{\mathbb{Z}} \to 0$$

Now read off the groups step by step:

$H_2(M,\partial M)$: From $0 \to H_2(M,\partial M) \to \mathbb{Z}$, the image of $H_2(M,\partial M)$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ equals $\ker({\times 2}) = 0$ (multiplication by 2 is injective on $\mathbb{Z}$). Therefore $H_2(M,\partial M) = 0$.

$H_1(M,\partial M)$: The map $\times 2: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$ has $\ker = 0$ and $\operatorname{im} = 2\mathbb{Z}$. By exactness at $H_1(M) = \mathbb{Z}$: $\ker(H_1(M) \to H_1(M,\partial M)) = \operatorname{im}(\times 2) = 2\mathbb{Z}$. So the image of $\mathbb{Z} \to H_1(M,\partial M)$ is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. And since the map $H_1(M,\partial M) \to H_0(\partial M)$ has image $\ker(H_0(\partial M) \to H_0(M)) = 0$ (the map $\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\cong} \mathbb{Z}$ is an isomorphism), the map $H_1(M,\partial M) \to H_0(\partial M)$ is zero. Therefore $H_1(M,\partial M) = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

The Central Result
$$H_n(M, \partial M) \cong \begin{cases} 0 & n=0 \\ \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} & n=1 \\ 0 & n=2 \end{cases}$$

Comparing with the cylinder: the vanishing of $H_2(M,\partial M)$ is the precise algebraic statement that the Möbius strip has no fundamental class — it is non-orientable. And the torsion group $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ in degree 1 is the algebraic shadow that the non-orientability leaves behind. It arises entirely from the factor of 2 in the map $i_*: H_1(\partial M) \to H_1(M)$, which itself comes from the half-twist.

The summary table

Cylinder $C$ $\mathbb{Z}$$\mathbb{Z}$$0$ $0$$\mathbb{Z}$$\mathbb{Z}$

Absolute homology cannot tell these surfaces apart. Relative homology can, and does so via the presence or absence of the fundamental class in $H_2(X,\partial X)$, and via the torsion $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ that appears precisely when the fundamental class is absent.

§7 Local Smooth Geometry is Blind ▾

Both the cylinder and the Möbius strip are smooth 2-manifolds with boundary. In the interior of either surface, every point has a neighbourhood diffeomorphic to an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$; near the boundary, every point has a neighbourhood diffeomorphic to a half-plane. The local smooth structure is identical for both surfaces.

The Riemannian picture

Place the flat metric $g = dx^2 + dy^2$ on the unidentified rectangle. This metric is invariant under the identification $(0,s) \sim (3, 1-s)$ (the identification is an isometry of the rectangle). So both the cylinder and the Möbius strip inherit a flat Riemannian metric.

In the flat metric:

  • Christoffel symbols $\Gamma^k_{ij} = 0$ everywhere (in any local coordinate chart)
  • Riemann curvature tensor $R^l{}_{kij} = 0$
  • Ricci tensor $\text{Ric}_{ij} = 0$
  • Scalar curvature $\kappa = 0$
  • Geodesics are straight lines (in local coordinates)

Every local geometric invariant — curvature, torsion of the connection, normal coordinates — is the same. No local measurement, however refined, can distinguish a patch of the Möbius strip from a patch of the cylinder. Smooth, local differential geometry is unable to detect non-orientability.

What about holonomy?

One might object: the Levi-Civita connection defines parallel transport, and parallel-transporting a frame around the core circle of the Möbius strip returns the frame with orientation reversed. Doesn't this detect non-orientability?

Yes — but parallel transport around a loop is a global computation. The holonomy group is defined by integrating the connection around closed curves, which requires knowledge of the topology of the surface (specifically, which curves are non-contractible). The connection itself — the Christoffel symbols — is flat and locally trivial. The holonomy "remembers" the global topology through the monodromy of the parallel transport: for the Möbius strip, the holonomy representation $\pi_1(M) \to O(2)$ sends the generator to a reflection, not a rotation. For the cylinder, the corresponding representation is trivial (or sends to a rotation in $SO(2)$).

So holonomy does detect the difference — but via global topology, not local geometry. It is, in fact, another face of the same non-triviality encoded in the homology.

The precise statement

Theorem (informal)

Let $U \subset M$ and $V \subset C$ be any open sets, each simply connected. Then $U$ and $V$ are diffeomorphic (as Riemannian manifolds, with the inherited flat metric). Every local invariant of differential geometry — curvature, connection, geodesics, normal coordinates, Taylor expansion of the metric — agrees on $U$ and $V$.

Non-orientability is a property of the global topology, not of any local structure. It is a statement about the first cohomology of the frame bundle with coefficients in $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, not about any local differential-geometric quantity.

§8 The Orientation Sheaf ▾

There is a clean algebraic-topological object that formalises the idea that orientability is "locally trivial but globally non-trivial." It is the orientation sheaf (or orientation local system) of the manifold.

Definition — Orientation Sheaf

For an $n$-manifold $M$, the orientation sheaf $\mathcal{O}_M$ is the locally constant sheaf (equivalently, the local system) whose stalk at $x \in M$ is

$$(\mathcal{O}_M)_x = H_n(M, M \setminus \{x\};\,\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}.$$

The transition maps between nearby stalks are determined by the local orientation: moving $x$ continuously, the stalk changes by a sign if and only if we cross a "orientation-reversing" path.

For the cylinder $C$: pick any loop in $C$. You can transport the orientation (a choice of generator $\pm 1 \in \mathbb{Z}$) around any loop and return with the same sign. The sheaf $\mathcal{O}_C$ is a trivial local system: $\mathcal{O}_C \cong \underline{\mathbb{Z}}_C$ (the constant sheaf). This is exactly orientability.

For the Möbius strip $M$: transport the orientation around the core circle $\alpha$. You return with the opposite sign — the monodromy of $\mathcal{O}_M$ around $\alpha$ is multiplication by $-1$. The sheaf $\mathcal{O}_M$ is a non-trivial local system; it is the unique non-trivial double cover of $S^1$ (the core circle), pulled back to $M$.

The monodromy representation

A locally constant sheaf with stalk $\mathbb{Z}$ on a path-connected space $X$ is equivalent to a group homomorphism $\rho: \pi_1(X) \to \text{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}) = \{\pm 1\} = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. This is the monodromy representation.

For the cylinder: $\pi_1(C) = \mathbb{Z}$, and $\rho_C: \mathbb{Z} \to \{\pm 1\}$ is the trivial homomorphism (every loop preserves orientation). For the Möbius strip: $\pi_1(M) = \mathbb{Z}$, and $\rho_M: \mathbb{Z} \to \{\pm 1\}$ sends the generator $1$ to $-1$. This is the unique non-trivial homomorphism $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

$$\rho_C: \mathbb{Z} \to \{\pm 1\},\quad n \mapsto +1 \qquad \text{(trivial)}$$ $$\rho_M: \mathbb{Z} \to \{\pm 1\},\quad n \mapsto (-1)^n \qquad \text{(non-trivial)}$$

Homology with twisted coefficients

Ordinary homology $H_*(M;\mathbb{Z})$ uses the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$. We can instead compute homology with coefficients in the orientation sheaf, $H_*(M;\mathcal{O}_M)$. This produces twisted homology, and the result is striking:

Space$H_2(X;\underline{\mathbb{Z}})$$H_2(X;\mathcal{O}_X)$
Cylinder $C$ (compact, with boundary)$0$$0$
Möbius Strip $M$$0$$\mathbb{Z}$
Closed orientable surface $\Sigma_g$$\mathbb{Z}$$\mathbb{Z}$
$\mathbb{R}P^2$$0$$\mathbb{Z}$

For the Möbius strip, $H_2(M;\mathcal{O}_M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$: a fundamental class exists}, but only in twisted coefficients. The twist "corrects" for the non-orientability, and the resulting class is the true orientation-invariant cycle. This is the cleanest algebraic formulation of the central theme: non-orientability is invisible to the constant sheaf (ordinary homology), but it is the very structure that $\mathcal{O}_M$ is built to detect.

The key picture

Locally, both $C$ and $M$ look like $\mathbb{R}^2$ — the sheaf $\mathcal{O}$ has stalk $\mathbb{Z}$ at every point, just like the constant sheaf. The difference is entirely in the gluing data: how these local copies of $\mathbb{Z}$ are assembled as you traverse loops. For $C$, the gluing is always the identity. For $M$, traversing the core circle applies a sign flip. This is what it means for a local system to be non-trivial.

§9 Synthesis ▾

We have traced a single geometric phenomenon — the half-twist of the Möbius strip — through several layers of algebraic topology. Let us draw the threads together.

The chain of causes

The identification $(0,s) \sim (3, 1-s)$ reverses the $s$-coordinate, creating the half-twist. This means:

  1. The boundary $\partial M$ is a single circle (versus two for the cylinder).
  2. Walking around $\partial M$ traverses the core circle twice: the inclusion $i_*: H_1(\partial M) \to H_1(M)$ is $\times 2$.
  3. The map $\times 2: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$ is injective, so $\ker(\times 2) = 0$, forcing $H_2(M,\partial M) = 0$.
  4. Its cokernel is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, producing the torsion in $H_1(M,\partial M)$.
  5. The monodromy of the orientation sheaf around the core circle is $-1$ (sign flip).

Why local geometry sees nothing

All of these phenomena are global. The half-twist is invisible if you zoom in to any single coordinate chart. Each chart sees only flat $\mathbb{R}^2$; each chart is individually orientable. The non-orientability is a property of how the charts are assembled — it lives in the transition maps, not in the charts themselves. The holonomy is the integral of the connection around a global loop; the orientation sheaf's monodromy is defined by analytic continuation along paths; the torsion in $H_1(M,\partial M)$ is computed from a global chain complex. All are manifestations of global topology.

Connections to singular learning theory

These ideas resonate with the SLT framework in a precise way. In SLT, the key object is the parameter space $W$ of a statistical model, which is a (typically singular) semi-algebraic set. The local structure of $W$ near a singularity is that of a real algebraic variety — locally, it looks like $\mathbb{R}^d$ with some algebraic relations. But the global topology of the singular set, measured by the RLCT $\lambda$ and by the homology of the resolution of singularities, encodes the true complexity of the model.

Just as local smooth geometry cannot detect the Möbius twist, local quadratic approximations (Fisher information geometry) cannot detect the deeper singularity structure that controls generalisation. The RLCT — a birational invariant, hence a global algebraic-geometric quantity — is the analogue of the fundamental class: present when the singularity is "orientable" (in the relevant algebraic sense), and reflecting a torsion obstruction when it is not.

What began as a question about a strip of paper — why does the twist disappear from local analysis? — resolves into a rich interplay between simplicial homology, exact sequences, sheaf theory, and the global topology of parameter spaces. The Möbius strip is not an exotic curiosity; it is a minimal model for the phenomenon that global topology encodes information that local geometry cannot access.

II (local systems and orientation sheaves). For the SLT connection: Watanabe, Algebraic Geometry and Statistical Learning Theory, Ch. 1–3.
